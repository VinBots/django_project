Fight against Climate Change Starts with Transparency (2/5)

Value Chain Emissions are Significantly Under-Reported

If ony the consumers could get in the game... before the regulator In the previous blog, we saw that only 1 out of 6 companies are fully transparent in their carbon emission reporting, even if about 4 out of 5 companies report scope 1-2 and a part of scope 3 emissions. The real game-changer is the full transparency on value chain (scope 3) emissions but today, I estimate that only 44% companies report all scope 3 emissions and only 16% get them fully verified by an external third-party.

Reporting value chain GHG emissions, both upstream (supply chain) and downstream (end-use), remains a major challenge for most corporates, in terms of data availability, accuracy, consistency, frequency and completeness. However, value chain emissions usually represent more than 80% total GHG emissions with some sectors well-above 90% (for example, 98% in consumer discretionary, 95% in financial services, 94% in consumer staples). MSCi recently published a study with similar findings (MSCI Net Zero Tracker), that shows the emissions intensity by scope and by sector and also tracks the progress in scope 3 reporting.

Among companies reporting some scope 3 emissions, I estimate that about 2/3 scope 3 emissions are omitted on average. The chart below helps visualize the gap by sector.

Yet, assessing the completeness of scope 3 emissions sounds like a judgement call. The relevance of scope 3 categories depends on the type of activities and even if CDP requires justification for every category of scope 3 emissions (section C6 of CDP questionnaire), companies within the same sector tend to have different approach or rationale to exclude some categories. This scope 3 completeness issue highlights the need for stronger sectorial guidance, methodologies and reporting framework standards specifically focused on scope 3 emissions. For example, banks tend not to report on scope 15 - Investments because of lack of widely-recognized methodologies; 3M does an excellent job at reporting their emissions but faces understandable challenges to report on product-level emissions (+50,000 products with various end-use); tech companies like Amazon and Apple are merging some scope 3 categories (IP reasons?). In summary: scope 3 emissions are significantly under-reported but there is hope as many companies are leading the way in adopting new tools and methodologies to not only demonstrate transparency but also disclose and optimize the full carbon footprint of their products and services. In terms of timing, I wish the consumers would get in the game before the regulators!
